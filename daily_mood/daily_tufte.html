Introduction

Starting in Mid-October 2024, I downloaded an app1 to wit, Dailybean; thanks tiktok that allows you to rate your mood once daily. I use an alarm at 8:30 pm to rate my mood each day. The app also allows you to indicate what you were feeling that day2 e.g.gloomy, happy, etc and what kinds of social interactions you had that day3 e.g.family, acquaintance, none. Starting in November, I began making a daily graph with a loess trend-line to see what the trend in my mood over time has been.

After about 120 days worth of ratings, I began to do deeper analyses on the data4 which are almost all binary or essentially qualitative descriptions to track trends in mood.

Beginning in February 2024, I turned the analyses I was running into a daily report that contains ongoing analyses so that after I input last nights data, I can get all of my typical questions re-calculated at the click of a button.

Daily Mood Over Time

How has mood changed over time, in general?5 In a previous version of this graph I jittered the points to give it more liveliness, but the jitters were obscuring the actual values of the ratings more than I wanted and adjusting them by functional arguments didnt solve the problem to my liking. In this plot, I have also marked significant days and holidays as annotations; e.g.my trip to Colorado to see Katrina and Arielle as well as Valentines day, etc. The unmarked line at the end of January is my coffee date with Ashley. The unmarked line at the beginning of March is the anniversary of Lauras text.

Is there a cyclical effect?

As of February 2024, there appears to be a cyclical or sinusoidal pattern to my mood ratings. In this version of the plot, I have taken each month as a different factor and plotted its loess smoothed line, along with adding a grey line that indicates the loess smoothing of all of the months in the data set. There does appear to be a weak pattern of rise and fall peaking around the 10th of every month and falling around the 20th, but thats probably just coincidences based on the fact that November and December both have holidays near the 20th of the month which depress scores.

More data are likely needed, at which point auto-regressive techniques might become necessary to determine real longterm patterns of growth.

The holiday effect

I lied before when I said holidays were strongly negatively associated with lower mood. This is an artifact in the data due to really bad times around Thanksgiving and Christmas. A t-test can be used to determine this effect. However, the t-test is so unbalanced8 the idea being that with currently 119 regular days and 6 holidays, it almost doesnt matter what the effect of holidays is, a t-test wont pick it up. Nevertheless! that the standard errors get blown to all hell and its worse than worthless. Some holidays suck, some holidays are anodyne. Nevertheless, Im going to keep it as a control for other models.
